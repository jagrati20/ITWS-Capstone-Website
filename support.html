Frequently Asked Question

​​WHICH CONDITIONS ARE TREATED WITH IMMUNOTHERAPY?
[Placeholder: Source = Facing Immunotherapy]​All cancers have found ways to avoid being targeted by the immune system. As a result, immunotherapy has been studied and used to treat almost every kind of cancer. Some immunotherapies work better for certain cancers than others; some cancers are very responsive to immunotherapy, while others are more resistant to existing treatments. Over the last several years, immunotherapy has also been helpful for tumors with certain genetic features, regardless of where the tumor originates.
​HOW EFFECTIVE IS IMMUNOTHERAPY?
[Placeholder: Source = Facing Immunotherapy]​Immunotherapy has varying effects on cancers.  ​For some diseases, immunotherapy can dramatically reduce the cancer size in a significant percentage of individuals, prolong survival, and may even cure a fraction of them. In other cancers, immunotherapy can reduce the cancer size and prolong survival, but may not able to cure the cancer entirely. The effects of immunotherapy depend on many factors, including the specific treatment being used, the type of cancer, and your own immune system.
​​HOW MUCH DOES IMMUNOTHERAPY COST?
​[Placeholder: Source = Facing Immunotherapy]​​Immunotherapy can be expensive; new drugs often cost more than $100,000 per year. Some treatments, such as CAR-T cell therapy, which involve collecting, modifying, and transfusing your own cells, can cost from $400,000 to $1,000,000 (once additional expenses like hospitalization are included)
​​WILL MY INSURANCE COVER THE COSTS OF IMMUNOTHERAPY AND ITS COMPLICATIONS?
[Placeholder: Source = Facing Immunotherapy]​Most insurance plans will cover the cost of immunotherapy if it is deemed medically necessary. However, there are often differences in the out-of-pocket costs for individuals; this depends on which plan you carry. For the most accurate information, you should speak to your oncologist and your insurance providers about your immunotherapy plan.
​​WHAT IS IN THE PIPELINE FOR IMMUNOTHERAPY?
​[Placeholder: Source = Facing Immunotherapy]​​Immunotherapy is a rapidly evolving field, with new treatments and new uses for those treatments coming out, seemingly, every month. Clinical trials are investigating new checkpoint inhibitors, monoclonal antibodies, adoptive T-cell therapies, vaccines, and engineered viruses. In addition to new treatments, oncologists are investigating combinations of existing immunotherapies, which may generate a more potent response to the cancer. Immunotherapies are also being combined with other more conventional treatments (like surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation therapy).
